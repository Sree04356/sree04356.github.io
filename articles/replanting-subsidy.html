Rubber Board Subsidy for Replanting

If you've never dealt with a government scheme before, here's what you actually need to know — not the official brochure language, the practical version.

The Rubber Board offers financial support when old, low-yielding trees are replaced with new ones. If you've inherited land with aging trees and don't know where to start, this is usually the first scheme worth understanding.

What it actually covers

The subsidy typically supports the cost of removing old trees and planting new saplings, released in stages tied to the plant's growth — not as one lump sum upfront. This matters because you shouldn't expect the full amount immediately.

What you'll likely need ready

  • Proof of land ownership or legal right to farm it (this trips up more people than anything else — if the land is still in a parent's or grandparent's name, sort this out first)
  • Your Rubber Board registration, if the land isn't already registered
  • Basic details of the current planting — how old, how many trees, approximate area

The realistic timeline

Approval and first disbursement often takes longer than officially stated — budget for months, not weeks, and follow up periodically rather than assuming silence means rejection.

The beginner mistake

Applying without registering the land with the Rubber Board first, if it isn't already. This single step causes more delays than any paperwork error.

Visit your nearest Rubber Board field office in person for the current application form — schemes and required documents do change, and a phone call or visit gets you the accurate, current version faster than searching online.
