When to Start Tapping This Season

If you've inherited rubber land, or you're managing it for aging parents, and you've never actually tapped a tree yourself — this is where to start.

Experienced tappers read a tree without thinking. If you're new to this, here's what they're actually looking for.

Bark recovery matters more than the calendar

A tree tapped last season needs its bark to heal before you cut again. Tapping too soon into unhealed bark can permanently reduce how much latex that tree ever produces — this is the single most common mistake beginners make, often without realizing it for years.

Don't tap during heavy, continuous rain

The latex gets washed away before it can be collected properly, wasting the cut entirely. Wait for a stretch of drier days, even if light rain continues on and off.

Tap early morning, always

Latex flow is highest in the cool hours and drops sharply once the sun is fully up — this matters more for yield than which week of the season you begin.

The mistake almost every beginner makes

Tapping the entire plantation on the same day, assuming all trees are on the same schedule. Trees mature and recover at different rates, especially if some were replanted more recently. Check tree by tree, not the whole plot at once.

If you're unsure whether a specific tree is ready, ask a family member or neighbor who's tapped before to check it with you the first time — a two-minute look can save a season's yield.
